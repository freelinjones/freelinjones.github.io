Graduate Papers

Jones, F. (2013). A Rock and a Hard Place: How Baltic Libraries Came Out the Other Side of Communism and How Russian Libraries Can Embrace the Freedom of Information. Durham, NC: North Carolina Central University.
Abstract: A look at the Baltic State libraries throughout history in comparison to their Russian counterparts.

Jones, F. (2013). Keeping Up with the Up Keep: The Challenges of Maintaining a Current Science and Technology Collection. Durham, NC: North Carolina Central University.
Abstract: A look at the challenges of maintaining current and up-to-date science and technology collections during a period of limited or reduced state funding in the modern academic library.

Jones, F. (2013). The Perception Gap: Measuring the Divide Between How Information Literate Undergraduate Students Think They Are and Their Actual Information Literacy. Durham, NC: North Carolina Central University.
Abstract: A proposed study in which the sole purpose is to create a theoretical framework to measure undergraduate students’ actual information literacy levels.

Jones, F. (2012). Undergraduate Students’ Uses of Online Information Sources: An Annotated Bibliography. Durham, NC: North Carolina Central University.
Abstract: Research suggests a move away from traditional information-seeking behaviors toward more virtual methods for the modern college student with 73% of students using the Internet more than the library when conducting academic research, while only a paltry 9% reversed that trend. The research in this annotated bibliography looks at studies that have attempted to answer the question: How do undergraduate students use online information sources?

Jones, F. (2012). Modern Challenges for the Modern Academic Library: Redefining the Academic Collection. Durham, NC: North Carolina Central University.
Abstract: Academic libraries are facing challenges, that while many of the challenges have been faced before, many are unique to this period in time. The three main challenges that will determine the direction of academic libraries are increased costs versus reduced funding, technology, and information literacy.
